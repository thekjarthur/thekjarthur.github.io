One Page Summary

We set out this semester wanting to create a yo-yo that looked like the front of the camera [1]. Our initial ideas included the camera body, a moving aperture, a lens and an LED light in the center of the yo-yo to add effect. In the end, we added a lens cap, we didn’t include the LED light, and the aperture got redesigned to a non-moving part. However, the end product does look like what we had in mind at the beginning of the semester [2].

Figure 1: Camera Inspiration, Figure 2: Final CAD YoYo Design

Starting with the body, it was designed primarily by David Doan [3]. It had to be significantly altered twice for multiple reasons, primarily to minimize dishing and to accommodate the insertion of the other parts of the yo-yo. A shim was also added to the production in order to increase the weight of the yo-yo to improve yo-yo functionality [4]. Getting the body to injection mold the way we desired proved difficult, but we managed to succeed shortly before the deadline.

Figure 3: Final Body Mold, Figure 4: Final Body with Shim

The original design of the aperture was scrapped near the beginning of the production process. Jade Hardacker had designed a moving aperture that was to be 3D printed. However, after 3D printing it, we decided that the surface finish was too rough and the production time too long [5]. A new design was made that was the equivalent shape of the previous design, but as one piece instead of eight separate moving parts. We chose to have one piece to injection mold instead of eight because of the difficulty it would take to create 3 molds and injection mold very thin pieces. The final aperture turned out great, and it was the first piece to finish its production run [6].

Figure 5: Original Aperture Design, Figure 6: Final Aperture Design

The lens and the lens cap were designed by Kwabena Arthur. The lens was altered only once, and getting it thermoformed did not provide many issues, although it was time consuming [7]. The bigger problem came in the form of the lens cap. After it was first injection molded it ended up being too big. Between shrinkage factor that was slightly off and the low tolerance of the press fit into the body, the entire lens cap mold needed to be altered and recreated [8]. This slowed down the team’s production, but Kwabena worked diligently to fix the problem.

Figure 7: Lens Mold and Final Product, Figure 8: Lens Cap Mold and Final Product

All of our parts came together on a late night as we all sat around a table and assembled our camera yo-yos [9]. The lenses were dirty, and the press fit inconsistent, but 50 yo-yos were assembled before sunrise [10].

Comparison to AM Service Bureaus (Shapeways, Fineline Prototyping etc)

Body

Using Shapeways, the cheapest and most appropriate 3D printing process available is powder bed and inkjet head printing. The material that can be used with this method is black, strong and flexible plastic. This plastic, when it layered thickly, can be strong and used for structural components. It is also the cheapest option to keep the yoyo below $20 per part. The quoted cost for 2 components is $37.52 and the for 100 components is $1876.

Aperture

Using Shapeways, it seems the most appropriate 3D printing process available for the aperture is also powder bed and inkjet heat printing. Using this method and black, strong and flexible plastic as the material, the aperture can be made relatively quickly and cheaply. For two components, the cost is estimated to be $12.20 and for 100 components it should be $610.

Lens Cap

Using Shapeways, it seems that the black, strong and flexible plastic through powder bed and inkjet heat printing is the best method to create the lens cap as well due to its relatively low price and manufacturing speed. To print two of these parts, it should cost $10.30 and to create 100 of them, it should cost $515.

Lens

To create the thermoformed piece using Shapeways, a white, strong and flexible plastic through powder bed and inkjet heat printing is the cheapest and fastest material solution. To create two lenses, it would cost $9.96, while 100 of them would cost $498.

Methodology

In order to calculate the per unit cost per yoyo, we need to break down the yoyo into its four main cost objects: (1) The material cost per yoyo, (2) the unit tooling cost, (3) the capital cost, and (4) the overhead cost.

Tooling Cost Assumptions

For the unit tooling cost we assume the following:

  1. Assume 100,000 cycles/mold
  2. Assume mold don’t have long cycle time (multiply initial tooling cost by 10 to estimate cost for a high cycle)
  3. Assume Cost of one mold tooling is $3000
  4. Assume costs are the same for the thermoform mold as for the IM mold.

Capital Cost Assumptions

For capital cost assumptions we assume the following:

  1. Assume we want to be producing 83,333 yoyo’s per month (1 million per year)
  2. Assume that the cycle time for injection molding (2 half bodies, aperture, lens cap)+ thermoforming + assembling one yoyo is (2min, 10sec, 10sec) + 30 sec + 4 minutes for assembly
  3. Lifetime of machine for the IM body, IM aperture, IM lens cap, and thermoform lens machine is each 10 years
  4. Assume all machines have 80% uptime (with 24 hours of operation)
  5. Assume an Integer number of machines will be in use
  6. Assume each process operates in parallel and does not depend on the quantity of yoyo

Overhead Cost Assumption

For the overhead cost, we assume the following:

  1. Assume a single worker can work 6 machines simultaneously at $60/hr
  2. Assume other overhead costs $6 per hour per machine
  3. Assume Each worker is on the job for 24hrs/day, cannot be hired part time, cannot be used for any other activity

Calculations

Material Cost

For the material cost per yoyo, we add up all of the costs of each individual part plus the cost for the material. This gives us:

C1 = .16 + .8 + .33 + .06895*(1.5)/(.2) + .00193*(1.12)/(.2)+2*1.15 = $4.12/unit

Where the cost for the material is given by the governing equation: =m*C_t/(1-e). This was used for both the injection molded part and the thermoform.

Tooling Cost

The tooling cost of the yoyo involves the cost of each of the molds. Since we assumed that all four molds were the same cost, we get that:

C2 = 4*10*3000/(100,000)*Int(100,000/100,000) = $1.2/unit

Capital Cost

Based off of an 80% uptime per machine per day, we get a machine that runs at 1152 minutes of uptime per machine per day. At this rate, and based off of the cycle times above, we can produce the following numbers of parts:

  • 576 bodies per machine day (this includes both halves) =210,240 bodies per machine year
  • 6912 apertures per machine day = 2,522,880 aperture per machine year
  • 6912 lens caps per machine day = 2,522,880 lens cap per machine year
  • 2304 lens per machine day = 840,960 lens per machine year

1 million yoyos =

  • 1 million bodies + (5 machines) $30,000 per machine = $150,000
  • 2 million apertures + (1 machine) $30,000 per machine
  • 2 million lens caps + (1 machine) $30,000 per machine
  • 2 million lenses + (2 machine) $20,000 per machine = $40,000

Results in =

Therefore the capital cost is:

C3 = 150,000/1,000,000+ 2*(30,000)/2,000,000 + 40,000/1,000,000= $0.22/unit

Overhead Cost

Explanation

The biggest cost component across the board, was overhead cost. There are a couple of reasons this could be the case. The first reason is that the cost of labor was assumed to be higher than it should have been, the second reason is that the cost of labor is fine, and that overhead cost is typically the dominating factor in manufacturing processes such as these.

Analysis of cross-over points in Case I & II

Ideally, this would occur at low volume production, hence the reason why AM is typically used at low volumes. However, our results indicate that the costs are higher at low volumes. The reason for this is most likely due to a possible error in assumptions: possibly in terms of time it take to make a part and costs associated with machines.

In the case where the production volume is high, we see that IM’ing will dominate in terms of lowest per unit cost. This is mostly due to cycle times associated with the production of a part. In our example we assumed it would take 9 hours to AM a full body of our yoyo, whereas an IM machine could do it in ~2 minutes. This has a huge competitive advantage at high volumes.

Final Analysis & Comparison with store bought YoYo

Attributes

Quality

The yoyo that was purchased on Amazon seems to be made with high quality in mind since it has few defects. The yoyo, as seen in the image below, has a smooth body that does not scratch easily. Its injection pin marks are not visible and only the nub on each half of the body that was connected to the runner proves how it was made. It also has embossed words compared with the Flash yoyo, which does not.

The Flash yoyo also seems to be of high quality with few defects, as shown in the following image. It too only has a nub from the runner to show that it came from an injection molded process.

Defects

The store bought yoyo could have a smaller nub from the runner. Its current one is very wide and still slightly protrudes from the body. The company could have spent more time trying to create a smaller tolerance for that part.

        The Flash yoyo also does not have many defects. It too could have had a smaller nub on the body to create a more uniform surface for the user. The center thermoformed piece also seems to get foggy and dirty, unlike the store-bought piece. It would have been useful if there was some sort of coat that could be applied to lens to make sure it does not get visibly dirty.

Size

The Flash yoyo is much bigger than the store bought Amazon yoyo. This means that this yoyo must have used more material (larger shot size) and had larger cooling time in order to get the larger size.

Cost

Like for size, the Flash yoyo used more material so it probably would have been more expensive compared store-bought yoyo. Also, since it is probably being made on the order of millions, the store-bought yoyo may be cheaper since its most of its processes would have been automatic.

Suggested Changes to Flash YoYo for Mass Production

To prepare the Flash yoyo for mass production, it could be modified slightly to increase number of sales. It could use a spray to help clean the lens.The yoyo could also be made with slightly smaller bodies. By shrinking the size of the yoyo, this could help reduce the price of material and make it more accessible to smaller children who would be willing to buy it.

Can Flash competitively sell their YoYo on Amazon?

As shown previously, the price to produce one of our yoyos is $7.16. Therefore, in order to make a profit, the price we feel would could sell our yoyo on Amazon while still remaining competitive with other products on the market is $10.00. This profit $2.84 (for each yoyo sold we make ~39% profit) can be used to improve the company and run research on how to develop the next product.
