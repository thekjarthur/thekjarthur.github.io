Abstract

The project consisted of the design and creation of an autonomous wheeled robot that would collect data in the form of images. The collected images would act as datasets and would be used to create neuronets in which testing for two long-term projects would take place. The robot is controlled through programming on an Arduino microcontroller, and using various functions of motors and sensors to autonomously drive. Platforms stemming from the base elevate the laptop computer, which controls the arduino and the camera together through serial communication. The camera setup is elevated even further to provide a field of view high enough up to produce the quality of pictures needed. For one project, the camera would take sets of five pictures at five different exposure times to produce HDR images, while for the other project, the cameras will take blurry images at different 2 different exposure settings. Overall, the setup is modifiable to contain either one or multiple cameras, and the programming can be modified as well to have the autonomous driving robot perform or execute different forms of movement.

 

Introduction

         The central concept behind the project was to obtain images to provide a sample neuro-net for testing of experimental technologies. One of these technologies can take images that are incorrectly saturated or have severe light exposure and recorrect them, leading to a variety of uses. If there is a dark spot in the image, the neuronet will be trained to take all the information from the dark spot that should be there. For the project, the start consisted of a base with wheels, and had to be built up from. The programming and design process had to be done from scratch, using various softwares such as Visual Studio, Arduino, and Autodesk.  It solved the present need of being able to automatically collect images. The robot still isn’t completely modifiable to be able to operate two cameras at once due to the software of the cameras, but it is a future possibility or function that could be added. Additionally, this feature would be able to lead to more uses for testing new technologies, such as a motion blur technology.

Approach

The approach to this project was built out of the goal of creating an autonomous driving robot that would be able to collect data in the form of images. The experimental setup took place in the first days of the project, when the robot was comprised of just the bottom base, the motors, Arduino, and the sensors. A lot of testing was done to attempt to have it track the wall. At the start, set values were set for the motor that depended on which range of distance away from the wall that the robot was: equal to 10, less than 10, or greater than 10. However, this way of autonomous driving was extremely inconsistent and not very efficient. Because of this, a change was made and the control switched to feedback error. The motor speed, or PWM, would change depending on the ultrasonic sensor transmission. If the robot was very close to the wall, the right motor would greatly increase its output, and as the robot strayed from the wall, the right motor’s PWM would decrease. If the robot got too far away from its track path, the same output would occur with the left motor. At first, this feedback error was used through the power mod, meaning that there would be a set base, and the sensor reading, called ClosestToWall, would be in the power. However, using power mod in the error feedback also resulted in inconsistencies in which the robot would stray too far away from the right wall, and a final switch was made to linear error feedback. Here, the ClosestToWall variable would just directly affect the PWM output of the motors, without bringing any other variables into play. The Arduino code for the motors and sensors that result in the autonomous driving can be seen in the Addendum.

Hardware

Base

The base is a 9 inch radius circle made of 1/2 inch thick wood. Attached to the base are two PWM motors that are controlled through a motor drive. The motors are each attached to  3 inch radius wheels that are on opposite sides of the base. On the front and back of the base are two smaller 1.5 inch radius office wheels that provide stability for the robot. In the middle of the base, there is a 14 inch long by 4 inch wide by 2 inch tall platform that houses the arduino and motor drive. Additionally, there are two sensors on the platform; one facing the right side, and the other one facing the left side. Back on the base, there are three more sensors: one facing the front, and two more sensors both facing 45 degrees out on each side. Wiring connects the ultrasonic sensors and motors to the arduino.

Frame

The frame consists of two additional platforms that stem from the base: a laptop platform and a camera platform. The laptop platform is attached to the base by four 7 inch metal posts. The metal posts were threaded and then connected by standard fasteners and washers. The laptop platform is also a 9 inch radius circle that is 1/4 inch thick. The platform contains a hole for any wires going to the arduino that can connect to the laptop. There is ample space for any other items or accessories that can be placed on this platform. There are three more metal posts rising from the laptop platform and connecting to the camera platform. These posts are each 18 inches tall, and the idea behind this was so that the cameras would be up high enough to be able to capture a good quality range in the image. Once again, the camera platform has a 7 inch radius and 1/4 inch thickness, and is attached to the posts by standard fasteners. On the camera base are two more stacked circles with a 5 inch radius. The bottom piece has holes in it to hold the screw heads in place and make sure they don’t stick out, allowing the two circles to rotate around on top of the camera base. There is an additional hole in the camera base that allows the wires from the camera to coil down onto the laptop platform and connect to the laptop.

Mounts

As the original design called for two cameras, mounts were designed and created for both the cameras and the beamsplitter. For 3D printing, the program AUTODESK Inventor was used for designing the different parts.

The original design for the camera featured a block to connect directly to the camera through the #8 fastener holes, and it extended down the camera to hold up the lens. The dimensions and design can be seen listed under Design 1 in the addendum. However, after using the Zortrax 3D printer for manufacturing, the design had structural issues and the part resulted in being warped. With the introduction of the beam-splitter into the system, a different solution was needed to comply with the dimensions of the entire platform.

However, after the introduction of the beam-splitter into the equation, a new, second design was needed.  The next idea was to design a singular piece that held the beamsplitter and the two cameras in one mount. However, due to the size of the cameras and beamsplitter, this design turned out to be too big, but was used for reference later in the dimensions of the holes to cut in the wood plates that the cameras and beam-splitter would sit on.

The third and final design that was ultimately settled upon was a simple block for the camera to sit on. This would help save material, as well as provide a simple and more efficient solution. The block is 0.5625 inches tall, and the length and width were determined by the data sheet for the camera. The holes to connect to the camera also came from the data sheet SEE Reference 1. At first, this mount was going to be machined using aluminum, but ended up being 3D printed. This would correspond with the new mount for the beamsplitter, which would stand by itself.  This piece was printed using the MarkForged Mark 2 3D printer using MarkForged Onyx material.

The idea behind the design for the beam-splitter was an X with raised corners so that the beam-splitter could neatly nestle into place. In the middle of the X, there is a circle that has space for a nut to sit in. This is used to secure the beam-splitter mount to the camera base. The beam-splitter fits in snuggly. To prevent it from falling out when the robot is moving, the beam-splitter can be lightly zip-tied to the cameras. This piece was printed using the MarkForged Mark 2 3D printer using nylon plastic material.

The assembly of the mounts onto the 5 inch radius circle plates required precise measurements so that the holes would fit perfectly, as well as the whole setup, being the cameras, staying straight. Using the previous and failed design in an attempt to connect the two cameras and beam-splitter together, the measurements and overall placement of the pieces was used to measure the holes in the piece of wood. After, the file with the measurements was uploaded as a dxf file to be able to be used in the laser-cutter. Since the laser cutter is only precise to two decimal points, the holes were a minimal gain off but the difference was indistinct and the setup was straight. The beam-splitter mount attaches to the singular hole at the front, while the camera mounts attach to the double-holes that face into the beam-splitter. The hole in the middle connects the two plates to the camera base and allows the functionality of being able to rotate the cameras if needed.

Electronics

Sensors

For the robot to be able to drive autonomously, ultrasonic distance sensors from MaxBotix were used SEE Reference 2. These sensors transmit and receive ultrasonic signals to measure distance. The design of the sensors allowed for them to measure imperial distance, though a simple mathematical operation had to be done to obtain the distance values. In this case, the conversion was to PWM (Pulse Width Modulation) signal received from the sensors by 2 to achieve the distance measured in inches. The sensors were daisy chained together in a series system, where we use one digital pin on the Arduino microcontroller to trigger a range reading of all the sensors on the robot, and receive the data with individual PWM pins on the microcontroller, each corresponding to a range sensor. In total, the robot uses five sensors: Right, Front Right, Front, Front Left, and Left. The front sensor is used to stop the robot from any obstacles that may appear in front of it. The two right sensors were used to track and follow the right wall of a path or hallway, and the left sensor was used to compare the distance to the right sensor and determine whether the robot would turn either left or right when the robot would arrive at the end of a path. In a different version of the code, the front left sensor can be used with the left sensor to track the left wall.

Image: Communication Between the Range Sensors and Microcontroller

        To control the robot through code, an Arduino UNO was used, and it was controlled through the programming language C #. In the setup, the Arduino controls everything, from the sensors to the motors to the cameras. The Arduino communicates with Visual Studio to operate the camera. The Arduino Reference page was used to comprise the functions and uses of the Arduino in the project SEE Reference 3.

Software

The robot uses two distinct pieces of software to operate, and the two work in conjunction with each other to produce a fluid working system. The first of the two is an Arduino C# program that directly controls the movement of the camera, and the second is a Visual Studio C++ Windows Console Application that operates the camera and captures the images. The Console Application was created using the ThorLabs SDK and SerialPort Class SEE References 4 and 5. The two communicate via a Serial COM port, in this case through a USB connection from the computer to the microcontroller. During operation, the Serial connection is used for the computer command the robot to stop so the camera can then take a set of images.

How to Use the Software

To use the robot in its intended manner, there are several steps to the process. First, make sure the drive battery is either disconnected or switched off. To initialize the drive system, connect the USB cable pre-connected to the microcontroller to the computer, and upload the Arduino program to the microcontroller. As you do so, please ensure that the robot is in a relatively open area and that the range sensors are not covered so the system and initialize properly. Next, open the C++ Console program, named “ThorCamAutoCapture” in Visual Studio as a project and enter/change the following variables and values to the specific details and values necessary:

  1. INTERVAL_TIME = Time robot Moves Between Each Set of Images (Milliseconds)
  2. CAPTURE_CYCLE_START = Starting Image Set Index - Used as Initial “for” Loop Index and for File Name When Saving Images (Warning: If Set Improperly Previous Images May Be Overwritten)
  3. CAPTURE_CYCLE_END = Ending Image Set Index - Used as Final “for” Loop Index and for File Name When Saving Images (Warning: If Set Improperly Previous Images May Be Overwritten)
  4. port_name = COM port the Arduino microcontroller is plugged into the computer (When Changing Port Name Don’t Change/Delete the Slashes)

After all of the above values are set for the specific case, and the Arduino program has been uploaded to the system, connect the camera via another USB port on the computer, run the C++ project, and plug in and switch on the battery to start the image collection process. The images will be stored in the repository of the C++ console program inside a folder also named “ThorCamAutoCapture”.

To convert the robot for multiple camera operations, first attach the second camera and the beam splitter to the system with their according mounts. So far in this project, controlling and capturing images or video on 2 cameras simultaneously on 1 computer or program has been unsuccessful. However, we believe it’s very much possible to do so, since the main obstacle so far for this process has been Thorlabs’ “Active Memory Assignment” Design in their SDK, yet in the native ThorCam Software it is possible to open and display live video from 2 cameras at the same time. As such, for now to control 2 cameras simultaneously, prepare 2 laptops, with each connecting to an individual camera. Since the dual camera functionality is used for the Motion Blur Project, the computers operating the cameras don’t have to communicate with the microcontroller controlling the movement of the robot to command the system to stop moving. Thus, there is no need for any Serial Communication between the computers and the microcontroller. To modify the program, simply remove all lines and sections in the C++ Program containing Serial Control and Communication so the cameras only run on a timed cycle. Additionally, instead of 5 pictures in each set, in this case only 1 is needed per cycle, and it is simply a matter of having a long exposure time on one of the cameras and a short exposure on the other.

Alternatively, a more efficient and effective way of doing this is only using one camera to capture a sequence of long and short exposure images. To do so, a second camera and a beam splitter would not be necessary. Also, similar to the previous method, serial communication wouldn’t be necessary since the robot shouldn’t stop to acquire images. In terms of the software programming, the only modifications would be to disable or remove all sections and lines of serial communication or commands from the Console Program, remove the last two image captures from the main capture loop so the camera captures three images per cycle, and change the exposure settings one, two, and three accordingly so that for each cycle, the camera acquires two long exposure images with one short exposure image in between. The Arduino program doesn’t need any modifications. When all of the above is completed, the robot is ready to operate. (See the beginning of “How to Use the Software” and setup the necessary variables and values as necessary) Upload the Arduino program to the microcontroller and wait for the range sensors to initialize. Then run the C++ console program and connect the battery to begin the image acquisition process. The images will be stored in the repository of the C++ console program inside a folder also named “ThorCamAutoCapture”.

Camera Operation and Programming

The Thorlabs DCC1240M Camera mounted on the robot is controlled and operated with a Visual Studio C++ Console Application. The program first establishes a connection with the Arduino microcontroller. After that, it initializes and prepares the camera for capture. This includes allocating image memory and assigning it as active memory. Next, it enters the capture loop with the number of cycles designated by the user. In the capture loop, the program first ensures the microcontroller is connected. If not, it would exit camera and exit program. Then it would send a command through the serial connection for the robot to pause and stop moving. After the program successfully reads the confirmation signal from the microcontroller, it waits one seconds and begins capture. The camera’s exposure time settings is configured to EXPOSURE_SETTING_1, and a picture is taken and saved with the name “Image_(Cycle_Count)_1”. This process is then repeated for all 5 exposure settings. After that, the program checks that the microcontroller is still connected, and sends the resume command for the robot to continue moving. The capture loop repeats from CAPTURE_CYCLE_START to CAPTURE_CYCLE_END for that designated number of cycles. After the capture cycle is completed, the program sends a terminate command to the microcontroller to stop the robot. In this case the Arduino program enters a forever constant while loop, where inside the loop it simply repeatedly sets the power for both wheels to 0. After that, the Visual Studio program exits the cameras, and reaches the end of the program.

Serial Communication

The Serial Communication between the Arduino microcontroller and the computer is implemented by using the SerialPort Class created by Manash Kumar Mandal SEE Reference 5  . Whenever the computer sends a command to the Arduino, it enters a flag variable controlled while loop and repeatedly sends the command through the serial COM port until the correct confirmation signal is received. The robot uses a baud rate of 115200 bits per second, and the COM port is designated by the user.

Firmware

Arduino

The Arduino code takes information from the sensors and using the code that was written, processes it and relays PWM outputs for the motors. The code fixates on the right wall, which the autonomous robot tracks as it is driving. If the value of the right sensors, called ClosestToWall, gets too close to the wall, the robot will start turning left. If the value of the right sensors gets too far away from the right wall, the robot will turn right. The Arduino code enables the robot to drive straight while adjusting to the wall it is tracking, as well as avoiding any obstacles in front of it.

Motor Calibration

As the project progressed and transitioned into feedback error for the control, the motors had to be calibrated to both find out the values to make the robot go straight, as well find out the different speeds that the motors can go at. The PWM of the motors ranges from 0 to 255, but due to the weight of the robot, it only starts moving at around 30. To find the speeds,  testing was first done on getting the robot to go straight, starting at 50 for the right motor and 46 for the left motor. After, straight-line control was tested, skipping 20 values each time, up to 220. After these values were obtained, a distance of 10 feet was measured out and the robot was timed going at the different PWM values as seen below. After, the distance was divided by the time and then converted to m/sec. These data values are useful if wanting to change the speed of the robot depending upon the project that is being used in correspondence with the robot; for example, if the goal is to obtain blurry pictures, a higher speed can be used.

Results

Image Collection

The results from our approach were successful, as a data set was collected from the testing and running of the robot. Although some images were corrupted due to light differences in different environments, enough images were taken and more images can be collected to obtain a greater data set. Different paths were taken through the buildings to obtain a variety of samples, as seen below. The overall performance of the autonomous driving robot was inconsistent but mostly positive. This would be one potential area of improvement for the project: the actual driving of the robot. The robot stopped and took pictures well and could follow the hallway, but there were occasional crashes during the image collection process. Lastly, as cycles of images taken included five pictures each, the environment that the camera was capturing needed to be completely still. However, due to people in the hallways, some cycles of images had to be scrapped as there was movement in each set of five images. One limitation that was noticed is that due to the slower speed of the robot but the recurring necessity of needing to collect enough images.

Conclusions

Listed below are the most important results summarized as a concise list, as well as possible future work.

  1. Serial Communication between Arduino and Visual Studio
  2. Design Process
  3. Controlling of Sensors and Autonomous Driving
  4. Camera Control
  5. Image Collection

Although functional and efficient, the autonomous driving robot is far from reaching its peak. For future work, to start, upgrades can be made to the sensors. Ultrasonic sensors aren’t the most effective as they can be affected by outside noise. Additionally, more than five sensors could be used to produce more accurate readings. Furthermore, the code that the arduino uses for driving the robot is inconsistent and could be improved if more research and time was put into it. Lastly, due to the limited functionality of the Thorlabs API, it is extremely difficult, if not impossible to simultaneously run two Thorlabs cameras at the same time. However, it might be possible and if found, one would be able to collect images using two cameras at the same time for different projects.
